The Density and Salinity of Water and How it Impacts Flooding

The coastal regions of the Philippines consist of bodies of water that mix fresh water with sea water (Paper). When typhoons occur and bring large amounts of rainwater, the significant amount of freshwater reduces the salinity and density in the bodies of water. Understanding the changes in the water is essential, as the salinity and density of water influences water flow and worsens flooding conditions.

Background

Bodies of water, especially those located in coast regions are naturally formed with layers of different water salinity. Saltwater is more dense than freshwater, and thus, tends to lie below freshwater layers. However, with typhoons bringing about intense rainfall, increasing freshwater levels, the mix of rainwater and salty seawater disrupt normal flow patterns (Article).

Development

As intense rainfall causes rainwater to flow into bays, the layers of water on the surface become significantly less dense because of the saltwater mixed in them. This salty seawater flows downwards through less dense freshwater layers, preventing bodies of water from efficiently mixing together. The denser saltwater remains on the bottom layer with the less dense freshwater above it, slowing the flow of water and preventing infiltration into soil.

Effects

The stratification caused by the mix of salinity slows the drainage of the flood, leading to faster flooding in coastal and urban areas of Philippines. The inefficiently mixed water drains into soil significantly slower, causing the floodwater to continue to accumulate with the intense rain. This change in the flow dynamic heavily impacts low-income communities in the coastal regions, where the infrastructure and drainage system are neglected by professionals, posing significant challenges in both social and environmental sustainability.

Study of Hurricanes and Typhoons in Philippines

The Philippines is positioned in one of the most typhoon-prone regions in the world, which results in the country frequently experiencing typhoons, which cause destructive winds and flooding that affect millions of people. Therefore, by understanding the development of typhoons and their destructive effects, we can more effectively implement solutions to mitigating their destruction. The following sections will describe what typhoons and hurricanes are, how they develop, and their effects.

Background

The most severe form of tropical cyclones, circular wind patterns, are known as hurricanes. They are a large, rotating storm where the winds move around a calm center. They have wind speeds of over 118 km per hour, which can cause extreme amounts of property damage. When they form over the north Pacific as in the Philippines, they are referred to as typhoons.

Development

The warm ocean water near the equator heats up and adds moisture to the air above it. Because the air molecules now have more kinetic energy, they expand, lowering the pressure. The surrounding air with higher pressure then rushes into the low pressure center, but because of the Coriolis effect, it deflects to the right, causing the storm to spin counterclockwise. The friction near the surface also reduces the effect of Coriolis and allows the air to tend to the low-pressure center instead of circling around it. The new air rises, causing the moisture to condense, which releases large amounts of latent heat into the storm. This causes the pressure to drop further, increasing the pressure gradient force, which causes more air to rush into the center, creating a positive feedback loop that intensifies the hurricane.

Effects

Because of the extreme pressure differential between the eye and the eyewall, air accelerates to the center and causes destructive winds of over 118 km per hour, which can destroy infrastructure like buildings and power lines. The strong winds also push ocean water toward the coast, which causes flooding. This is also exacerbated by the low-pressure center creating a rising dome of water, which effectively raises the sea level.

Three Air Cells

The physical location of the Philippines on Earth plays a role in its being flood-prone. To understand this, we first have to explain the three convection cells of Earth that impact the climate of a region based on the latitude. Both the Northern and Southern hemispheres can be divided into three major convection cells, as shown in Figure 1. These are driven by uneven solar distribution received by the Earth and play a major role in creating climate zones, as will be explained. As seen from Figure 1, there are three convection cells: the Hadley, Ferrel and Polar cells. These will be further explained below.

The Hadley cell spans from the Equator (0° latitude) to the 30° latitude mark. The Equator receives the highest amount of solar energy on Earth, causing air to be heated and to rise, creating a low pressure zone. This air is moist, so when it increases in elevation, it will cool and condense, creating heavy rainfall. Areas near the Equator are thus covered by an area called the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone, where storms frequently occur. The now cooled and dry air will move forward to the 30° latitude, where it sinks creating a high pressure zone. As a side note, these create dry conditions at this latitude (like the Sahara desert) and the pressure gradient causes the air pockets to travel back towards the equator, completing the convection cell.

The Philippines having a latitude of 12° North belong inside the Hadley cell, explaining it’s heavy rainfal (shown in Figure 2). Briefly describing the other two cells, the Ferrel Cell has sinking air at the 30° latitude and then rising air at the 60° latitude, due to meeting the colder air from the Polar cell, which is referred to as the Polar Front. Since the Poles receive the least solar energy, the air is extremely low and sinks, creating a high pressure zone forcing air to the lower pressure zone at the 60° latitude, where the air rises to complete the cell.
